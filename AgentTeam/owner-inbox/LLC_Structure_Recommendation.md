## Executive Summary

Given the diversity of Stephen's six business entities — spanning physical services, product sales, consulting, and digital content — the recommended structure is **two to three operating LLCs**, potentially with a **holding company** added later once revenues justify the administrative overhead. A Series LLC under Indiana Code 23-18.1 is worth discussing with an attorney but carries meaningful caveats that make it a secondary option rather than the default recommendation.

The priority is getting **YourStory Home Design** and **I Got a Guy Indy / Time to Shine** legally protected now. The content and consulting entities can follow in a planned sequence over the next 6–12 months.

## 1. Recommended Structure

### Option A: Two Operating LLCs + Future Holding Company (Recommended)

**LLC 1 — Services & Trades**
- I Got a Guy Indy (handyman)
- Time to Shine Mobile Auto Spa (mobile detailing)

These two entities share a similar liability profile (physical work performed on client property/vehicles), similar insurance requirements, and similar operational rhythms. Grouping them reduces filing and administrative overhead. The key risk of grouping: a significant lawsuit against one could theoretically expose the assets of the other if they share an LLC. Maintaining clean financial separation within the LLC (separate bank accounts, separate books) mitigates but does not eliminate this.

**LLC 2 — Creative & Product**
- YourStory Home Design (custom furniture)
- Retooling for Retirement (YouTube + content brand)
- TBD 3D Printing & Engineered Product Sales

These share a creative/maker identity and some operational overlap (Stephen's workshop, tools, time). YourStory's product liability and content brand's reputational liability are different in character but both relatively lower risk than physical service work. The 3D Printing entity, once active, fits naturally here given the Bambu Lab/xTool sponsorship context of Retooling for Retirement.

**LLC 3 — Consulting (Standalone)**
- Inspiration @ Innovation (Mechanical Design & Engineering Consulting)

This stands alone. Consulting work for other businesses or engineering clients carries a distinct liability profile, often involves contracts with IP and indemnification clauses, and may have professional liability (E&O) insurance requirements. Mixing it with consumer-facing entities creates unnecessary complexity and potential cross-contamination of liability. Keep it clean and separate.

**Holding Company (Future — Year 2+)**
A holding company (a parent LLC that owns membership interests in the operating LLCs) makes sense when revenues are substantial enough to justify moving profits upward, when you want a centralized asset ownership structure, or when you are doing real estate or investment activity. At Stephen's current stage — transitioning, building revenue — the administrative cost (separate bank account, separate returns, operating agreement, annual reports) is not worth it yet. Revisit when annual combined revenue exceeds $150–200K or when a specific asset protection need arises.

### Option B: Indiana Series LLC

Indiana enacted Series LLC legislation under **Indiana Code 23-18.1** (effective July 1, 2018). A Series LLC allows a single master LLC to contain multiple "protected series," each with its own assets, liabilities, and members — theoretically insulating each series from the others.

**Why it is appealing here:** One filing, lower annual overhead, internal separation of liability across all six business lines.

**Why to approach with caution:**

1. **Federal tax treatment is unresolved.** The IRS has not issued final guidance on how Series LLCs are treated for federal tax purposes. Depending on the approach taken, each series may need its own EIN and may be treated as a separate entity — which largely eliminates the simplicity argument.

2. **Banking is difficult.** Most major banks will not open separate accounts for individual series. This undermines the practical liability firewall.

3. **Cross-state recognition is inconsistent.** If Stephen does consulting or product sales outside Indiana, other states may not recognize the series structure, potentially treating the whole master LLC as one entity.

4. **Case law is thin.** Indiana's Series LLC law is relatively new. There is limited legal precedent on how Indiana courts will treat the liability separation in a real dispute.

**Verdict:** Discuss with your attorney. It is not the default recommendation here, but if your attorney is experienced with Indiana Series LLCs and the banking issue can be resolved, it could simplify the structure long-term.

### Option C: Fully Separate LLCs (Six Entities = Six LLCs)

Maximum liability isolation. Maximum administrative burden: six annual reports, six registered agents (or one registered agent covering all six, which is manageable), six bank accounts, six operating agreements, six EINs.

At Stephen's stage, this is over-engineered. Revisit individual separations as specific entities grow.

## 3. Indiana-Specific Guidance

### Formation Fees

| Item | Cost |
|------|------|
| Articles of Organization (online) | $95 per LLC |
| Expedited processing (24-hour) | Additional $50 |
| DBA / Assumed Business Name filing | $30 per name |
| Annual Report filing fee | $32 per LLC per year |

**Source:** Indiana Secretary of State Business Services Division (inbiz.in.gov). Fees current as of early 2026 — verify before filing.

### Annual Report Requirements

Indiana LLCs must file a **Business Entity Report** every two years (not annually — every other year). The filing window opens in the anniversary month of formation. The fee is $32 (online) or $50 (paper). Failure to file results in administrative dissolution after a grace period. Set a calendar reminder.

**Update (verify with SOS):** As of recent Indiana legislative changes, confirm whether the biennial report schedule still applies or has been updated to annual. Your attorney or the SOS website will have current information.

### Registered Agent Requirements

Every Indiana LLC must maintain a registered agent with a physical Indiana address (P.O. boxes are not sufficient). Options:

1. **Stephen serves as his own registered agent** — free, but his home address becomes a matter of public record and he must be available during business hours. Fine for most sole-proprietor situations.
2. **Hire a registered agent service** — typically $50–$150/year per LLC. Services like Northwest Registered Agent, Registered Agents Inc., or ZenBusiness are widely used. Using one service for all LLCs reduces cost and keeps Stephen's home address private. Recommended if Stephen is concerned about privacy or frequently works away from home.

### Indiana-Specific Considerations

- **Single-Member LLCs in Indiana** are recognized and provide personal liability protection, but they are "disregarded entities" for federal tax purposes (income flows to Schedule C) unless an S-corp election is made.
- **No state income tax on LLCs at the entity level** — Indiana taxes flow-through income at the individual level (flat rate of 3.05% as of 2026; verify current rate).
- **Indiana does not require an operating agreement to be filed**, but it is strongly recommended to have one drafted and signed, even for single-member LLCs. It is essential if Stephen ever adds a partner or applies for business credit.
- **Contractor licensing:** I Got a Guy Indy may trigger Indiana's home improvement contractor registration requirements. Check with the Indiana Attorney General's office and the Indiana Professional Licensing Agency.
- **Sales tax:** YourStory and the 3D Printing entity selling physical goods must register with the Indiana Department of Revenue for a Retail Merchant Certificate (free) and collect Indiana sales tax (currently 7%) on sales to Indiana customers.

## 4. Tax Structure Implications

### Default Tax Treatment

A single-member LLC is a **disregarded entity** — income is reported on Schedule C of Stephen's personal return. No separate federal return required. All net income is subject to **self-employment tax (15.3%** on the first ~$168,600 of net earnings; 2.9% above that — verify current thresholds with your CPA).

A multi-member LLC is taxed as a **partnership** by default — files Form 1065, issues K-1s to each member.

### S-Corp Election Consideration

An S-corp election (Form 2553) causes the LLC to file as an S-corporation for federal tax purposes. The primary benefit: **self-employment tax savings**. As an S-corp, Stephen would pay himself a reasonable salary (subject to payroll taxes), and any remaining profit distributed above that salary is **not** subject to self-employment tax.

**When this matters:** The tax savings typically justify the added complexity (payroll, quarterly payroll tax deposits, Form 1120-S filing) when net profit from the entity exceeds approximately **$50,000–$80,000 per year**. Below that threshold, the payroll accounting costs often erase the savings.

**Transition timeline implications:** Stephen is 1–2 years from exiting corporate work. Several critical considerations:

1. **W-2 income from corporate job affects overall tax picture.** While employed, Stephen's self-employment income is taxed on top of his salary. High combined income could push him into higher brackets. A CPA needs to model this for his specific situation.
2. **ACA health insurance.** If Stephen loses employer-sponsored health insurance, he will likely purchase coverage through the marketplace. LLC income structure affects ACA subsidy eligibility — this is a meaningful planning variable. A CPA with ACA experience should run these numbers.
3. **S-corp election timing.** If Stephen expects YourStory or the Services LLC to generate $50K+ net profit within the next 1–2 years, it may make sense to make the S-corp election at formation or in the first tax year. Elections made after March 15 of the tax year take effect the following year. Do not wait — plan ahead.
4. **Retirement accounts.** LLCs taxed as S-corps can sponsor Solo 401(k) plans, SEP-IRAs, or SIMPLE IRAs. The S-corp structure affects maximum contribution calculations. Given Stephen's "Retooling for Retirement" context, maximizing retirement contributions during high-income transition years is worth prioritizing.
5. **QBI deduction.** Qualified Business Income deduction (Section 199A) allows up to 20% of qualified business income to be deducted on the personal return for pass-through entities. Most of Stephen's entities should qualify (consulting income may have limitations depending on how the IRS classifies mechanical design/engineering). A CPA should confirm QBI eligibility per entity.

## 6. Questions to Bring to Your Attorney and CPA

These are the five most important unresolved questions given the complexity of Stephen's situation. Do not file anything until you have clarity on at least 1–3.

**1. Should the Services LLC (I Got a Guy / Time to Shine) use a Series LLC structure under Indiana Code 23-18.1, given the high liability exposure, or does the banking limitation make a true Series LLC impractical?**

Your attorney will know whether Indiana Series LLCs are being used successfully in practice for businesses like this, and whether local banks are now accommodating series-level accounts. This could change the entire recommended structure.

**2. What is the optimal S-corp election timing for YourStory Home Design given Stephen's 1–2 year corporate exit timeline, and how does it interact with ACA subsidy eligibility in the first year post-employment?**

This is a modeling question for your CPA. The answer depends on projected LLC profits, Stephen's expected corporate severance or final W-2 income, ACA household income thresholds, and retirement contribution goals. Run the numbers before the election.

**3. Does Inspiration @ Innovation's mechanical design and engineering consulting qualify as a Specified Service Trade or Business (SSTB) under IRS rules, which would limit or eliminate the QBI deduction?**

Engineering is specifically listed as an SSTB-adjacent field. Depending on how the IRS classifies mechanical design consulting, Stephen's QBI deduction on that entity could be phased out at higher income levels. A CPA needs to flag this before he structures the entity's income.

**4. What contractor licensing, bonding, or insurance requirements does Indiana impose on I Got a Guy Indy given the types of handyman services performed?**

Indiana has registration requirements for certain home improvement contractors, and violations carry penalties. Your attorney can confirm whether the services performed triggered registration requirements and whether any past exposure needs to be addressed.

**5. If the Retooling for Retirement content brand generates significant revenue through sponsorships, should it eventually be separated into its own LLC or treated as a separate profit center within LLC 2?**

Sponsor relationships with large companies (Bambu Lab, xTool, Harvey Tools) will involve contracts, deliverables, and potential IP licensing. If the brand grows materially, your attorney should assess whether it warrants its own entity for IP ownership and liability isolation — particularly if a co-host, editor, or business partner is ever added.
